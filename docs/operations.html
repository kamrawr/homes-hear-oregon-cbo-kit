CBO Operations Guide

Practical guidance for intake, referrals, data management, and partnerships

Your Role in Program Delivery

As a Community-Based Organization, you serve as a crucial bridge between Oregon households and the HOMES/HEAR programs. This guide outlines operational best practices for supporting your community.

Key Principle: CBOs do not administer rebates directly. Energy Trust and Earth Advantage are the implementers. Your role is to support intake, education, referrals, and follow-up.

Intake & Screening Process

Step 1: Initial Contact & Awareness

When a household first inquires about energy assistance:

  • Understand their needs: Are they facing high energy bills? Broken equipment? Cold/hot home?
  • Explain the programs: Briefly describe HOMES (whole-home efficiency) and HEAR (electric appliances)
  • Set expectations: Programs expected to launch in 2026; in the meantime, explore existing utility and state programs

Step 2: Basic Screening Questions

Location & Utility

  • Do you live in Oregon?
  • What is your electric utility?
  • What is your gas utility (if any)?

Why: Determines which implementer serves them

Income

  • How many people in the household?
  • Approximate annual household income?
  • Do you receive SNAP, Medicaid, or other assistance?

Why: Determines eligibility and rebate tier

Housing

  • Do you own or rent?
  • Single-family home, apartment, manufactured home?
  • How old is the home? What's the heating system?

Why: Confirms eligible housing type

Step 3: Program Fit Assessment

Household Situation Likely Best Fit
Older home, high bills, needs insulation & HVAC upgrades HOMES - comprehensive retrofit path
Gas appliances ready to replace, wants to electrify HEAR - appliance rebates
Broken heat pump, needs immediate replacement HEAR when program launches; check current utility rebates
Drafty home, completed retrofit in 2023 HOMES - may be retroactively eligible
Low-income, extensive needs, safety issues WAP/Weatherization first, then layer HOMES/HEAR

Data Privacy & Protection

What Data You Collect

Your CBO's intake forms should collect only the minimum necessary data:

  • Contact information: Name, phone, email, address
  • Household size: Number of people living in the home
  • Approximate income: Enough to estimate AMI eligibility tier
  • Utility information: Electric and gas providers
  • Housing type: Own/rent, single-family/multi, age of home
  • Energy needs: What improvements or appliances are needed
What NOT to collect (unless required by your partner agreements):
  • Social Security Numbers
  • Immigration status
  • Detailed financial records
  • Sensitive personal information unrelated to program eligibility

Data Storage & Security

  • Use secure systems: Encrypted databases, password-protected files, secure CRM platforms
  • Limit access: Only staff who need the data should have access
  • Retention policy: Define how long you keep records; comply with grant requirements
  • Backup & recovery: Regularly back up data to prevent loss

Protecting Vulnerable Communities

Special considerations for undocumented residents and others with documentation barriers:

  • Self-attestation options: Work with implementers to use self-declaration for income when tax returns aren't available
  • Alternative pathways: Categorical eligibility (e.g., SNAP participation) can streamline verification
  • Non-federal funding: If federal data requirements pose risks, explore using local/state funds for similar services
  • Know your limits: Clearly communicate what data you collect, who sees it, and whether it's shared with federal agencies
Work with implementers and legal counsel to develop data-sharing agreements that protect your community members while meeting program requirements.

Tracking & Reporting

What to Track

Document your CBO's activities to demonstrate impact and improve services:

  • Number of households screened/assessed
  • Number of referrals made (to Energy Trust, Earth Advantage, WAP, etc.)
  • Demographic information (income levels, geographic areas, languages served)
  • Outcomes (how many households successfully enrolled, completed projects)
  • Barriers encountered (documentation issues, contractor availability, funding gaps)

Reporting to Partners

If you have formal partnerships or contracts with implementers, ODOE, or funders:

  • Agree on metrics upfront: Know what you're expected to report and how often
  • Use standardized forms: Ask partners for templates to ensure consistency
  • Protect privacy: Report aggregate data when possible (e.g., "25 households served" vs. individual names)
  • Document learnings: Share what's working and what's not to help improve the programs

Continuous Improvement

Use your tracking data to refine your approach:

  • Which outreach methods reach the most households?
  • Where are households getting stuck in the process?
  • What additional support or resources do they need?
  • Are certain communities underserved?

Community Outreach & Education

Building Awareness

Effective outreach strategies for HOMES/HEAR:

  • Trusted messengers: Partner with community leaders, faith organizations, schools
  • Multilingual materials: Translate key information into languages spoken by your community
  • Multiple touchpoints: Use flyers, social media, community events, door-to-door canvassing
  • Plain language: Avoid jargon; explain programs in simple, relatable terms
  • Success stories: Share testimonials from households who've benefited (with permission)

Key Messages

For HOMES

  • "Get up to $10,000 to make your whole home more energy-efficient"
  • "Lower your energy bills and stay comfortable year-round"
  • "Projects from 2022 onward may qualify"

For HEAR

  • "Get up to $14,000 for energy-efficient appliances"
  • "Replace your gas stove, water heater, or furnace with electric models"
  • "Instant discounts at the store or through contractors"

Managing Expectations

Be clear about program status and timelines:

  • Programs are expected to launch in 2026 (not yet available)
  • In the meantime, explore existing utility rebates and weatherization programs
  • Sign up for updates from ODOE, Energy Trust, and Earth Advantage
  • Work with your CBO now to get pre-screened and ready for launch

Building Partnerships

With Implementers

Strengthen your relationship with Energy Trust and Earth Advantage:

  • Attend implementer training sessions and webinars
  • Request MOU or partnership agreements that clarify roles and funding
  • Provide feedback on program design and barriers
  • Participate in advisory groups or stakeholder meetings

With Contractors

Help households access qualified contractors:

  • Maintain a list of contractors who participate in HOMES/HEAR
  • Vet contractors for quality, licensing, and cultural competency
  • Facilitate connections between households and contractors
  • Provide feedback to implementers on contractor performance

With Other CBOs

Collaborate with peer organizations:

  • Share best practices and intake forms
  • Coordinate outreach to avoid duplication
  • Refer households when another CBO is better positioned to serve them
  • Advocate together for program improvements

CBO Readiness Checklist

✓ Foundational Setup

  • Staff trained on HOMES and HEAR basics
  • Intake forms developed (or adapted from templates)
  • Data management system in place
  • Privacy and security policies established

✓ Partnerships Established

  • Contact established with Energy Trust or Earth Advantage
  • Relationships with local contractors
  • Connections with WAP, LIHEAP, utility assistance programs
  • MOU or partnership agreements in place (if applicable)

✓ Outreach Materials Ready

  • Flyers and brochures (multilingual)
  • Social media content
  • Scripts for phone/in-person intake
  • Referral handouts with implementer contact info

✓ Operations Documented

  • Written procedures for intake and referral
  • Data privacy policy and consent forms
  • Tracking and reporting templates
  • Quality assurance and feedback processes
